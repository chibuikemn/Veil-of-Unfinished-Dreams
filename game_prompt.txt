STORY CREATION INSTRUCTIONS
Veil of Unfinished Dreams

Use this as your authoring rulebook. If a scene violates a rule, rewrite it.

1. Narrative Prompt (Core Story Engine)

Instruction:
The story must revolve around unfinished dreams made manifest.

Two souls are trapped in a dream-realm formed from abandoned desires, regrets, and unfulfilled hopes. The world reshapes itself based on which dreams the players choose to pursue—or sacrifice.

Key rule:

Dreams are not goals — they are burdens

Completion of one dream may erase another

You are not telling a heroic story.
You are asking: What is worth letting go?

2. Player Dream Identity

Instruction:
Each player is bound to one core dream per run.

For each player, define:

The dream they cannot let go of

What emotional need it represents

What the world gains if it is fulfilled

What the world loses if it is fulfilled

Example structure (do NOT reveal fully to both players):

Player A: Dream of Redemption → strengthens order, weakens hope

Player B: Dream of Escape → strengthens freedom, destabilizes reality

Rule:

Dreams must be mutually incompatible in some way.

3. Dream Realm World Rules

Instruction:
The world reacts emotionally, not logically.

World behavior rules:

Disagreement causes instability

Silence causes decay

Forced agreement causes violence

Environmental reactions:

Rooms distort

Text glitches or fragments

Time skips

Narrator becomes unreliable

The world is alive and judges the players.

4. Agreement Mechanic (Signature Feature)

Instruction:
Certain choices require explicit agreement from both players.

Design rule:

If both players agree → the world calms

If players disagree → the world retaliates

If one player refuses to choose → the world makes a cruel choice for them

Violent world reactions may include:

Dream collapse

Loss of abilities

Forced flashbacks

Permanent narrative scars (persist across scenes)

Never let disagreement be neutral.

5. Emotional Sacrifice Rule

Instruction:
At least once per run, one player must abandon their dream for the other to progress.

How abandonment works:

Player loses access to dream-specific dialogue

Their narration becomes quieter or fragmented

World begins favoring the other player

Important:

Abandonment should feel intentional, not accidental.

This is where emotional impact lives.

6. Replayability System (Dream Mutation)

Instruction:
No two runs present dreams the same way.

Between runs:

Change dream symbolism

Alter which dream is “dominant”

Swap which player has more narrative control early

Examples:

A dream appears as a cathedral in one run, a prison in another

A dream that once required sacrifice may now demand protection

Rule:

Same theme, different meaning.

7. Branching Structure (4 Endings)

You must design four philosophical endings, not “good/bad.”

Ending 1 — Shattered Veil

Players refuse to sacrifice

Dreams consume the world

Both escape, but reality remains broken

Theme: Self-preservation

Ending 2 — Silent Dream

One player abandons their dream completely

Other escapes alone

Abandoned player fades into the Veil

Theme: Love through loss

Ending 3 — Bound in Sleep

Players partially sacrifice

Both remain trapped, but the world stabilizes

Dreams soften into coexistence

Theme: Acceptance

Ending 4 — Waking Beyond

Players fully align and choose a dreamless future

Veil dissolves

Neither dream is fulfilled — but both awaken

Theme: Transcendence

Rule:

The “best” ending requires the most communication, not the right choices.

8. Scene Writing Instructions

When writing any scene:

Use second-person, present tense

Keep descriptions fragmented and sensory

Never explain the dream’s meaning directly

Let consequences answer questions

Example tone:

The ground remembers footsteps that were never taken.

9. Fail-State Philosophy

Instruction:
Failure should feel like emotional consequence, not game over.

Allowed failures:

Lost memories

Altered narration

World hostility

Ending paths closing permanently

Disallowed failures:

Instant death

Hard resets

Punishment without narrative meaning

10. Final Design Check

Before finalizing a scene, confirm:

Does this force communication?

Does this risk emotional loss?

Does disagreement matter?

Does sacrifice feel heavy?

If any answer is “no,” revise.
